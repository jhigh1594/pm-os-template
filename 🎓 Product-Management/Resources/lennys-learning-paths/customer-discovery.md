# Customer Discovery & Research - Learning Path

> Curated episodes for Sr. PMs who need to master customer research, Jobs-to-be-Done methodology, and turning customer conversations into actionable product decisions. **Portable skills that apply across any product or market.**

## Learning Objectives

After completing this learning path, you will be able to:

1. **Design and conduct effective customer interviews** - Ask questions that uncover real needs, not stated preferences
2. **Synthesize patterns across customer conversations** - Turn scattered insights into actionable themes
3. **Turn insights into product decisions** - Balance customer input with vision, data, and constraints
4. **Apply Jobs-to-be-Done framework** - Understand why customers hire your product and what job they're trying to get done
5. **Build customer obsession across your organization** - Share insights in ways that build customer empathy

## Prerequisites

- **Knowledge**: Basic understanding of product management and customer research concepts
- **Experience**: Any level—from PMs just starting customer research to those looking to deepen their practice
- **Time**: 3 hours (6 episodes × 30 minutes focused listening)

**Career Focus**: Customer insight is your strategic superpower. The PMs who advance to Director and VP roles are the ones who deeply understand customers and can translate that understanding into strategy and influence.

## Curated Episodes

### Episode 1: How to Find Work You Love - [Bob Moesta](../lennys-podcast-transcripts/episodes/bob-moesta/transcript.md)

> **Guest**: Bob Moesta, Co-creator of Jobs-to-be-Done Framework and Author of "Demand-Side Sales 101"
> **YouTube**: [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2wypVv9wZtI](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2wypVv9wZtI)
> **Relevance**: Bob shares the Jobs-to-be-Done framework—the definitive approach to understanding why customers buy and how to uncover real needs

**Key Timestamps**:
- `08:45` - What is Jobs-to-be-Done?
- `22:30` - The four forces of progress: push, pull, anxiety, habit
- `38:15` - Interview techniques for uncovering jobs
- `52:00` - Why customers can't tell you what they want
- `1:06:30` - Synthesizing insights from customer interviews

**Key Insights**:
1. **Customers Hire Products to Do a Job**: People don't buy products—they hire them to make progress in their lives. Your job as a PM is to understand what job they're trying to get done, why they're struggling with current solutions, and what would make them switch.
2. **The Four Forces of Progress**: Push (pain with current solution), Pull (attraction of new solution), Anxiety (fear of the new), Habit (comfort with the old). People switch when Push + Pull > Anxiety + Habit. Understanding these forces helps you design products and messaging that overcome barriers to adoption.
3. **Interview for the Struggle, Not the Solution**: Don't ask customers what they want—they'll tell you feature requests that won't actually solve their problem. Ask about their struggles: "Tell me about the last time you tried to [do this job]. Walk me through what happened." The struggle reveals the real need.

**Practice This Skill**:
> **Jobs-to-be-Done Interview**: Conduct a customer interview using Bob's framework. Ask about the last time they tried to do the job your product addresses. Map their struggle, the forces at play, and what progress they were trying to make.

**Discussion Questions**:
1. What job are your customers trying to hire your product to do? Have you actually asked them?
2. What's the struggle with current solutions that creates demand for your product?

### Episode 2: Customer-Led Growth - [Gia Laudi](../lennys-podcast-transcripts/episodes/gia-laudi/transcript.md)

> **Guest**: Georgiana Laudi, Co-founder of Forget The Funnel and B2B SaaS Growth Expert
> **YouTube**: [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0FgZ1VVxEBo](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0FgZ1VVxEBo)
> **Relevance**: Gia shares how to do customer research in B2B SaaS contexts and turn insights into growth strategies

**Key Timestamps**:
- `06:30` - What is customer-led growth?
- `19:45` - Customer research in B2B: how it differs from B2C
- `34:15` - Jobs-to-be-Done applied to B2B SaaS
- `48:00` - Interview techniques for B2B customers
- `1:02:30` - Turning customer insights into product strategy

**Key Insights**:
1. **B2B Research is Multi-Person Research**: In B2B, your "customer" isn't one person—it's a buying committee. You need to research users, buyers, and champions separately. Each has different jobs and different constraints. Ignore this and you'll build features users love but buyers won't pay for.
2. **Customer Research is Ongoing, Not One-Off**: Great product teams talk to customers every week, not just during annual research projects. These conversations build customer intuition over time. You're not looking for statistical significance—you're looking for pattern recognition.
3. **Share Insights Broadly**: Customer research only matters if it shapes product decisions. Share insights with engineering, design, sales, and customer success. Use customer stories, not just data. Stories build empathy; data builds awareness.

**Practice This Skill**:
> **B2B Stakeholder Mapping**: For your product, map the buying committee. Who are the users? Who are the buyers? Who are the champions? What job is each person trying to get done? Plan customer interviews with each type.

**Discussion Questions**:
1. When was the last time you talked to a customer? When was the time before that?
2. Who's on your buying committee? Have you interviewed each type separately?

### Episode 3: 10 Lessons on Bootstrapping a $200M Business - [Patrick Campbell](../lennys-podcast-transcripts/episodes/patrick-campbell/transcript.md)

> **Guest**: Patrick Campbell, Founder of ProfitWell and Paddle
> **YouTube**: [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FjLSCrSg5QY](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FjLSCrSg5QY)
> **Relevance**: Patrick shares why most companies fail at customer research and how to do it right

**Key Timestamps**:
- `07:30` - Why companies fail at customer research
- `21:00` - The user research philosophy that actually works
- `36:45` - Turning research into actionable insights
- `51:15` - Pricing research: how to discover willingness to pay
- `1:05:30` - B2B customer research best practices

**Key Insights**:
1. **Most Customer Research is Waste**: Companies ask customers what features they want, customers say everything's important, companies build everything, nothing works. This isn't research—it's a waste of time. Real research uncovers problems, not solutions.
2. **Research Questions, Not Answers**: Don't ask customers to solve your problems—ask them about their problems. "What's the hardest part of [doing this job]?" "Walk me through the last time you encountered [this problem]." Let them teach you their world.
3. **Quantify the Pain**: Great research doesn't just identify problems—it quantifies them. How often does this problem occur? How severe is it? What workarounds are people using? Quantification helps you prioritize which problems are worth solving.

**Practice This Skill**:
> **Problem Quantification**: For your top 3 customer problems, quantify: (1) How many customers experience this? (2) How often does it occur? (3) How severe is it (1-10)? (4) What workarounds are they using? Use this to prioritize your roadmap.

**Discussion Questions**:
1. What's the most painful problem your customers face? How do you know? Have you quantified it?
2. What workarounds are customers using? What do those workarounds tell you about the problem?

### Episode 4: Why Most Product Managers Are Unprepared - [Casey Winters](../lennys-podcast-transcripts/episodes/casey-winters/transcript.md)

> **Guest**: Casey Winters, Former CPO at Eventbrite and Growth Expert
> **YouTube**: [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WlRfyEpAKxw](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WlRfyEpAKxw)
> **Relevance**: Casey shares how to balance customer input with product vision and data

**Key Timestamps**:
- `05:45` - The customer research trap
- `19:30` - When to listen to customers vs. when to lead
- `34:00` - Balancing customer input with vision and data
- `48:15` - Customer research in growth contexts
- `1:02:00` - Turning customer insights into strategy

**Key Insights**:
1. **The Customer Research Trap**: Some PMs only do what customers ask for (feature factory). Others ignore customers entirely and build from vision. Both are wrong. Great PMs use customer research to understand problems, then apply their own creativity and expertise to design solutions.
2. **When to Listen vs. When to Lead**: Listen to customers about problems—they're the experts on their experience. Lead on solutions—you're the expert on what's feasible and what will work. If customers tell you what to build, you're doing it wrong.
3. **Customer Research + Data + Vision**: The best product decisions integrate all three. Customer research tells you what problems matter. Data tells you where the opportunities are. Vision tells you what's possible and what fits your strategy. Ignore any of the three at your peril.

**Practice This Skill**:
> **Three-Lens Decision Framework**: For your next product decision, map: (1) What do customers say (research)? (2) What does the data say (analytics)? (3) What does vision say (strategy)? How do these three perspectives align or conflict?

**Discussion Questions**:
1. When was the last time you built something customers asked for that didn't actually solve their problem? What went wrong?
2. How do you balance customer input with your own product vision?

### Episode 5: Customer Discovery and Opportunity Solution Trees - [Teresa Torres](../lennys-podcast-transcripts/episodes/teresa-torres/transcript.md)

> **Guest**: Teresa Torres, Author of "Continuous Discovery Habits" and Product Discovery Coach
> **YouTube**: [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wO1vBJKBGvY](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wO1vBJKBGvY)
> **Relevance**: Teresa shares how to turn customer conversations into actionable product decisions using opportunity solution trees

**Key Timestamps**:
- `04:30` - What is continuous discovery?
- `18:15` - Opportunity solution trees explained
- `32:45` - Turning customer insights into tree structure
- `47:00` - Using the tree to guide product decisions
- `1:01:30` - Integrating discovery into your weekly routine

**Key Insights**:
1. **Discovery is Continuous, Not episodic**: Great product teams talk to customers every week, not just during "research sprints." These ongoing conversations build customer intuition and feed a steady stream of insights into product decisions.
2. **Opportunity Solution Trees**: Start with the desired outcome (e.g., "increase conversion"). Map the opportunities to achieve that outcome (e.g., "reduce friction in signup"). For each opportunity, map solution ideas. For each solution, map assumptions to test. This structure turns customer insights into actionable experiments.
3. **Test Assumptions, Not Solutions**: Before building anything, test the assumptions behind your solution. "Will users understand this feature?" "Will they value it enough to use it?" "Can we build it?" These quick tests prevent wasted work on solutions that won't work.

**Practice This Skill**:
> **Opportunity Solution Tree**: Pick one product outcome you're trying to improve. Map the opportunities to achieve that outcome based on your customer conversations. For each opportunity, list 2-3 solution ideas. For each solution, list 2-3 assumptions to test.

**Discussion Questions**:
1. When was the last time you talked to a customer? Be honest—if it's been more than 2 weeks, you're not doing continuous discovery.
2. What assumptions are you making about your current product initiatives? How could you test them quickly?

### Episode 6: Product Discovery, Innovation Accounting, and Shipping Less - [John Cutler](../lennys-podcast-transcripts/episodes/john-cutler/transcript.md)

> **Guest**: John Cutler, Product Discovery Expert and Former PM at AppFolio
> **YouTube**: [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qP5BVRfShrI](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qP5BVRfShrI)
> **Relevance**: John shares how to balance customer discovery with the pressure to ship and how to avoid building things nobody wants

**Key Timestamps**:
- `08:00` - The discovery vs. delivery trap
- `22:30` - Innovation accounting: measuring what matters
- `37:45` - When to stop discovering and start building
- `51:15` - Customer research in time-constrained environments
- `1:05:30` - Building less but delivering more value

**Key Insights**:
1. **Discovery vs. Delivery is a False Choice**: Many teams treat discovery and delivery as sequential: first we research, then we build. This is wrong. Great teams do both continuously: small discovery experiments alongside delivery work. Discovery feeds better delivery decisions; delivery informs what to discover next.
2. **Innovation Accounting**: You can't measure discovery the same way you measure delivery. Delivery is about output (features shipped). Discovery is about learning (assumptions tested). Track discovery metrics: number of customer interviews, assumptions tested, hypotheses invalidated. These measure your discovery velocity.
3. **Shipping Less but Delivering More**: The goal isn't to ship more features—it's to deliver more value. Customer research helps you build the right things. If research prevents you from building one feature that nobody wants, it's paid for itself many times over.

**Practice This Skill**:
> **Discovery Metrics**: Track your discovery work for one month: (1) Number of customer interviews, (2) Number of assumptions tested, (3) Number of hypotheses invalidated. If interviews < 4 per month, you're not doing continuous discovery.

**Discussion Questions**:
1. How much time do you spend on discovery vs. delivery? Is the balance right?
2. What's one feature you're building that customer research could have saved you from building?

## Synthesis: Cross-Episode Themes

### Theme 1: Understand Problems, Not Solutions

**What the experts agree on**: Bob Moesta, Gia Laudi, and Patrick Campbell all emphasize that customers can't tell you what to build—they can only tell you about their struggles. Your job is to understand the problem, then design the solution.

**Portable Skill**: In every customer conversation, focus on the struggle, not the solution. Ask "What's hard about this?" not "What would you like us to build?" The struggle reveals the real opportunity.

**Career Application**: Directors and VPs are hired for their customer insight. Build deep customer intuition now, and it will pay dividends throughout your career.

**Key Quotes**:
> "People don't buy products—they hire them to make progress in their lives." — Bob Moesta

> "Don't ask customers to solve your problems—ask them about their problems." — Patrick Campbell

### Theme 2: Discovery is Continuous, Not Episodic

**What the experts agree on**: Teresa Torres, John Cutler, and Gia Laudi all reinforce that great product teams talk to customers every week, not just during "research sprints."

**Portable Skill**: Commit to 2-3 customer conversations per week. These don't need to be formal interviews—coffee chats, user testing sessions, support call shadowing all count. Build customer intuition over time.

**Career Application**: The PMs who advance to leadership roles are the ones who truly understand customers. Continuous discovery is how you build that understanding.

**Key Quotes**:
> "Discovery is continuous, not episodic. Great teams talk to customers every week." — Teresa Torres

> "Discovery and delivery aren't sequential—they're both continuous." — John Cutler

### Theme 3: Customer Insight is Your Strategic Superpower

**What the experts agree on**: Casey Winters, Teresa Torres, and Gia Laudi all point out that customer insight is what differentiates great PMs from good ones—and it's the foundation of strategic influence.

**Portable Skill**: After every customer conversation, write down: (1) What problem did they struggle with? (2) How often does it occur? (3) How severe is it? (4) What does this tell us about our strategy? Share these insights broadly.

**Career Application**: Customer stories are your most influential communication tool. When you can tell a compelling customer story, stakeholders listen. Customer insight is your path to influence.

**Key Quotes**:
> "Customer research only matters if it shapes product decisions. Share insights broadly." — Gia Laudi

> "Customer stories build empathy; data builds awareness. You need both." — Teresa Torres

## Action Plan: Applying This to Your Work

### Week 1: Start Your Customer Conversation Practice
- [ ] **Schedule 3 customer conversations**: Reach out to customers directly or join sales/support calls. Don't wait for permission—just do it.
- [ ] **Practice Jobs-to-be-Done interviewing**: Ask about the last time they tried to do the job your product addresses. Map their struggle and the forces at play.
- [ ] **Share one insight**: After each conversation, write down one key insight and share it with your team. Stories build empathy.
- **Output**: Three customer conversations and a habit of continuous discovery

### Week 2: Build Your Opportunity Solution Tree
- [ ] **Pick one outcome**: Choose a product outcome you're trying to improve (e.g., increase conversion, reduce churn).
- [ ] **Map opportunities**: Based on your customer conversations, map 5-10 opportunities to achieve that outcome.
- [ ] **List solutions**: For each opportunity, list 2-3 solution ideas you could pursue.
- [ ] **Identify assumptions**: For each solution, list 2-3 assumptions you should test before building.
- **Output**: An opportunity solution tree that guides your product decisions

### Week 3: Test One Assumption Quickly
- [ ] **Pick one high-impact assumption**: From your tree, choose one assumption that, if wrong, would waste significant work.
- [ ] **Design a quick test**: How can you test this in < 1 week with < $1k? Mockups, landing pages, customer conversations—anything that gives you signal.
- [ ] **Run the test**: Execute your test and gather data.
- [ ] **Share what you learned**: Share your findings with the team, regardless of outcome. Learning is progress.
- **Output**: One validated (or invalidated) assumption and a culture of experimentation

## Career Progression: From Sr. PM to VP Product

**Customer insight is your strategic superpower.** Here's how customer research skills scale with seniority:

| Skill | Sr. PM | Director | VP Product |
|-------|--------|----------|------------|
| Research Frequency | 2-3 conversations/week | 5-10 conversations/week | Continuous, plus broad customer immersion |
| Research Scope | Your product or feature | Multiple products or customer segments | Entire customer base and market |
| Insight Application | Features and roadmap | Product strategy and positioning | Company strategy and market direction |
| Storytelling | Team and immediate stakeholders | Cross-functional leadership | Company-wide and external (board, customers, press) |

**Your path to VP**:
1. **Sr. PM**: Build deep customer intuition for your product. Be the person who truly understands your customers.
2. **Director**: Expand your customer understanding across products and segments. Be the person who can synthesize patterns across the portfolio.
3. **VP**: Use customer insight to drive company-level strategy. Be the person who brings the customer voice into every strategic conversation.

**Start now**: The customer relationships and insight you develop as a Sr. PM are the foundation for Director and VP roles. Every customer conversation builds your strategic superpower.
