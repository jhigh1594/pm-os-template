# Team & Org Design - Learning Path

> Curated episodes for Sr. PMs who need to hire great PMs, design product org structures that scale, and develop the organizational leadership skills required for Director and VP roles. **Portable skills for any product leadership position.**

## Learning Objectives

After completing this learning path, you will be able to:

1. **Hire and grow great product managers** - Apply proven frameworks for assessing PM talent and making hiring decisions
2. **Design product org structures that scale** - Organize teams for autonomy, alignment, and effectiveness at scale
3. **Manage and develop PM teams** - Provide feedback, coach growth, and build healthy team cultures
4. **Navigate organizational change and reorgs** - Lead through change while maintaining team focus and morale
5. **Build healthy team cultures** - Create environments where PMs do their best work and grow

## Prerequisites

- **Knowledge**: Understanding of PM roles and product team dynamics
- **Experience**: 5+ years in product roles, currently managing PMs or preparing for people management
- **Time**: 5 hours (8 episodes × 35-45 minutes focused listening)

**Career Focus**: Technical and strategic skills get you hired—organizational leadership skills get you promoted to Director and VP. The difference between Sr. PM and VP is your ability to design and lead organizations.

## Curated Episodes

### Episode 1: Designing Your Product Development Process - [Gokul Rajaram](../lennys-podcast-transcripts/episodes/gokul-rajaram/transcript.md)

> **Guest**: Gokul Rajaram, Product Leader at Google, Facebook, Square, DoorDash
> **YouTube**: [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TkVuW0D4ujE](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TkVuW0D4ujE)
> **Relevance**: Gokul shares comprehensive frameworks for hiring great PMs, designing product orgs, and building product development processes

**Key Timestamps**:
- `06:30` - When and how to hire your first PM
- `22:45` - A playbook for hiring PM leaders
- `38:15` - Designing product org structures
- `52:00` - Product development process design
- `1:06:30` - Building culture and rituals

**Key Insights**:
1. **Hire for Trajectory, Not Just Experience**: When hiring PMs, don't just look at what they've done—look at how they've grown. The best PMs are the ones who learn fast and adapt. Ask about their biggest mistakes and what they learned. That tells you more than their successes.
2. **The 70-20-10 Rule for PM Hiring**: 70% of your PMs should be solid performers who execute reliably. 20% should be high-potential stars who can take on bigger roles. 10% should be exceptional leaders who can build and lead teams. Get this balance wrong and your org will struggle.
3. **Org Design Clarity**: Every PM should know: (1) What problem they're solving, (2) Who their stakeholders are, (3) What decisions they own, (4) How their success will be measured. Ambiguity in any of these areas creates organizational friction.

**Practice This Skill**:
> **Hiring Framework Audit**: For your next PM hire (or for your current team), map each PM against: (1) Execution capability, (2) Strategic thinking, (3) Leadership potential, (4) Growth trajectory. Identify gaps and build your hiring bar.

**Discussion Questions**:
1. What's your PM hiring bar? Can you articulate the 3-4 things every PM on your team must be able to do?
2. Does every PM on your team have clear problem ownership and success metrics? If not, what ambiguity exists?

### Episode 2: From Managing People to Managing AI - [Julie Zhuo](../lennys-podcast-transcripts/episodes/julie-zhuo/transcript.md)

> **Guest**: Julie Zhuo, Former VP Product at Facebook and Author of "The Making of a Manager"
> **YouTube**: [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8Z8nDqfH7cU](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8Z8nDqfH7cU)
> **Relevance**: Julie shares modern management skills and how AI is changing team structures and leadership

**Key Timestamps**:
- `07:30` - The transition from IC to manager
- `23:45` - Management skills that matter now vs. what mattered before
- `39:15` - How AI is changing team structures and roles
- `54:00` - Small team dynamics and leadership
- `1:08:30` - Building healthy team cultures in the AI era

**Key Insights**:
1. **The IC-to-Manager Transition is Hard**: The skills that made you a great PM (autonomy, execution, problem-solving) won't make you a great manager. Management is about enabling others, not doing it yourself. Your success is measured by your team's output, not yours.
2. **Management is Context-Dependent**: Great managers adapt their style to the person and situation. Some PMs need hands-on guidance. Others need autonomy and air cover. Some situations require decisiveness. Others require collaboration. One style doesn't fit all.
3. **AI Changes Team Composition**: AI doesn't replace PMs—it changes what PMs do and how teams are structured. Smaller teams can do more. Technical skills matter less; judgment and taste matter more. The best PMs will be the ones who can direct AI effectively.

**Practice This Skill**:
> **Management Style Audit**: For each person you manage, assess: (1) How much guidance do they need? (2) What motivates them? (3) Where are they growing? (4) What management style works best for them? If you don't know, ask them.

**Discussion Questions**:
1. What's your management philosophy? Can you articulate it clearly?
2. How has AI (or could AI) change how your team works? Are you adapting your leadership approach accordingly?

### Episode 3: The Things Engineers Are Desperate for PMs to Understand - [Camille Fournier](../lennys-podcast-transcripts/episodes/camille-fournier/transcript.md)

> **Guest**: Camille Fournier, Former CTO at Rent the Runway and Engineering Leadership Expert
> **YouTube**: [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jkX0yK8fZgY](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jkX0yK8fZgY)
> **Relevance**: Camille shares the engineering perspective on PM collaboration and org design

**Key Timestamps**:
- `08:15` - What engineers wish PMs understood
- `24:30` - PM-engineering collaboration models
- `40:00` - Technical debt: how PMs should think about it
- `55:15` - Structuring product and engineering organizations
- `1:10:00` - Building healthy PM-engineering relationships

**Key Insights**:
1. **Engineers Want PMs Who Understand Technical Constraints**: You don't need to be technical, but you need to understand technical implications. "Can we do this?" is the wrong question. "What are the technical implications of this?" is the right one. Engineers respect PMs who think about technical tradeoffs.
2. **Technical Debt is Real Debt**: Every shortcut creates interest payments in the form of slower development, more bugs, and reduced flexibility. Great PMs understand these implications and balance short-term wins with long-term health. Sometimes you take on debt—make that decision explicitly, not accidentally.
3. **Org Design Enables Collaboration**: The best PM-engineering collaboration comes from clear org design. Each product area should have a dedicated PM-engineering pairing. They should report into different functions but work as a tight team. Frequent reorgs destroy this collaboration.

**Practice This Skill**:
> **PM-Engineering Collaboration Audit**: For each product area, assess: (1) Is there a clear PM-engineering pairing? (2) Do they have shared goals? (3) How often do they communicate? (4) What's the quality of their collaboration? Identify one area to improve.

**Discussion Questions**:
1. What technical implications does your current roadmap have? Have you discussed them with engineering?
2. Is technical debt accruing in your product? What are you doing about it?

### Episode 4: The Rituals of Great Teams - [Shishir Mehrotra](../lennys-podcast-transcripts/episodes/shishir-mehrotra/transcript.md)

> **Guest**: Shishir Mehrotra, Co-founder of Coda and Former Head of Product & Engineering at YouTube
> **YouTube**: [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9LKO0w3VpR0](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9LKO0w3VpR0)
> **Relevance**: Shishir shares how to build team rituals that create alignment and velocity

**Key Timestamps**:
- `05:45` - What are team rituals and why they matter
- `21:30` - Rituals for alignment and communication
- `38:00` - Rituals for decision-making
- `52:15` - Rituals for growth and learning
- `1:06:45` - Scaling rituals as teams grow

**Key Insights**:
1. **Rituals Create Culture**: Culture isn't values on a wall—culture is what you do repeatedly. Rituals are the repeated practices that embody your culture. Great teams have rituals for communication (standups, updates), decision-making (reviews, retros), and growth (1:1s, feedback).
2. **Rituals Must Serve a Purpose**: Don't have rituals because you're supposed to. Have rituals because they solve a problem. What problem does this ritual solve? If you can't answer that, kill the ritual. Bad rituals waste time and breed cynicism.
3. **Rituals Scale with Thought**: As teams grow, rituals that worked at 10 people break at 50. The standup that kept everyone aligned becomes the meeting that wastes everyone's time. Scale rituals by adjusting frequency, format, and participants—not by abandoning them entirely.

**Practice This Skill**:
> **Ritual Audit**: List every recurring ritual on your team (meetings, updates, reviews). For each, ask: (1) What problem does this solve? (2) Is it still the best way to solve that problem? (3) Who actually needs to participate? Kill one ritual that doesn't serve a clear purpose.

**Discussion Questions**:
1. What rituals does your team have? Do they serve clear purposes?
2. Which ritual would your team miss most if it disappeared? That's your most important ritual—protect it.

### Episode 5: Why Most Product Managers Are Unprepared - [Casey Winters](../lennys-podcast-transcripts/episodes/casey-winters/transcript.md)

> **Guest**: Casey Winters, Former CPO at Eventbrite and Growth Expert
> **YouTube**: [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WlRfyEpAKxw](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WlRfyEpAKxw)
> **Relevance**: Casey shares how to structure growth teams and design orgs for velocity

**Key Timestamps**:
- `08:00` - Why PMs are unprepared for real startups
- `24:30` - Growth team structures that work
- `40:15` - Embedding PMs in engineering teams
- `55:00` - Designing orgs for autonomy and alignment
- `1:09:30` - Scaling product organizations

**Key Insights**:
1. **PMs Need End-to-End Ownership**: The worst org structures fragment responsibility across multiple PMs. "This PM owns features, that PM owns backend, this PM owns integration." This creates finger-pointing and slow decision-making. Great orgs give PMs end-to-end ownership of customer outcomes.
2. **Growth Teams Need Dedicated Structure**: Growth work requires different org design than core product. Growth teams should be small (2-3 engineers, 1 PM, 1 designer), focused on one funnel stage, and measured by north star metrics. Don't scatter growth work across existing teams—create dedicated growth squads.
3. **Autonomy Requires Alignment**: You can't have autonomous teams without clear strategic direction. Great orgs balance autonomy (teams decide how to solve problems) with alignment (leadership decides which problems to solve). Too much autonomy without alignment = chaos. Too much alignment without autonomy = bottleneck.

**Practice This Skill**:
> **Org Design Audit**: Map your current org structure. For each PM, ask: (1) What outcome do they own end-to-end? (2) What decisions can they make without approval? (3) What strategic direction guides their work? If outcomes or decision rights are unclear, redesign the structure.

**Discussion Questions**:
1. Does every PM on your team own clear outcomes end-to-end? If not, what's fragmented?
2. Does your org balance autonomy and alignment well, or is it skewed one direction?

### Episode 6: Product Strategy, OKRs, and Building a Product Org - [Anneka Gupta](../lennys-podcast-transcripts/episodes/anneka-gupta/transcript.md)

> **Guest**: Anneka Gupta, Former VP Product at Airbnb
> **YouTube**: [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RxzK7OYb3QA](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RxzK7OYb3QA)
> **Relevance**: Anneka shares how to design product orgs that execute strategy through OKRs

**Key Timestamps**:
- `06:30` - Connecting strategy to org structure
- `22:00` - OKRs that actually work
- `38:45` - Designing orgs around objectives
- `54:15` - Managing managers and scaling leadership
- `1:08:30` - Reorgs: when and how to do them

**Key Insights**:
1. **Org Design Follows Strategy**: Don't design your org in isolation. Start with your strategy: what problems are we solving? What outcomes matter? Then design orgs to execute that strategy. If strategy changes, org design should follow. Strategy without org alignment is just a document.
2. **OKRs Connect Strategy to Execution**: Good OKRs cascade from company strategy to team objectives. Each team's OKRs should connect clearly to business outcomes. Bad OKRs are feature lists disguised as objectives. Good OKRs are measurable outcomes that align teams to strategy.
3. **Reorgs Are Expensive and Sometimes Necessary**: Reorgs create disruption, lost productivity, and relationship damage. Don't reorg casually. But sometimes reorgs are necessary to align org with strategy, break down silos, or fix structural problems. When you reorg, over-communicate the rationale and give people time to adjust.

**Practice This Skill**:
> **Strategy-to-Org Audit**: Write down your product strategy. Then map your current org structure. Does every team's work connect clearly to the strategy? If there are teams whose work doesn't align, either change the strategy or change the org.

**Discussion Questions**:
1. Does your org structure clearly reflect your product strategy? Where are the misalignments?
2. When was the last time you reorged? Was it necessary? Did you over-communicate the rationale?

### Episode 7: Product Discovery and Shipping Less - [John Cutler](../lennys-podcast-transcripts/episodes/john-cutler/transcript.md)

> **Guest**: John Cutler, Product Discovery Expert and Former PM at AppFolio
> **YouTube**: [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qP5BVRfShrI](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qP5BVRfShrI)
> **Relevance**: John shares how to structure teams for effective discovery and delivery

**Key Timestamps**:
- `09:30` - The discovery-delivery imbalance
- `25:00` - Structuring teams for continuous discovery
- `41:45` - Team composition and roles
- `57:15` - Managing discovery teams
- `1:12:00` - Scaling discovery practices

**Key Insights**:
1. **Discovery Requires Team Structure**: Discovery won't happen if it's just "something PMs should do." Great teams structure for discovery: dedicated time, clear processes, and shared ownership. The whole team participates in discovery, not just the PM.
2. **Optimal Team Size for Discovery**: Small teams (2-3 engineers, 1 PM, 1 designer) can do effective discovery together. Large teams fragment discovery across sub-teams, creating inconsistent insights and duplicated work. Keep teams small and focused.
3. **Discovery Management is Different**: Managing discovery teams requires different skills than managing delivery teams. You're managing for learning, not output. You're coaching curiosity, not checking off tasks. You're celebrating invalidated assumptions, not just shipped features.

**Practice This Skill**:
> **Discovery Structure Audit**: For each product team, assess: (1) Is there dedicated time for discovery? (2) Does the whole team participate, or just the PM? (3) Is discovery managed differently than delivery? If the answer to any of these is "no," redesign the structure.

**Discussion Questions**:
1. Does your team structure enable continuous discovery, or does it treat discovery as episodic?
2. How do you manage discovery differently than delivery?

### Episode 8: How to Build a Meaningful Career - [Jason Shah](../lennys-podcast-transcripts/episodes/jason-shah/transcript.md)

> **Guest**: Jason Shah, Former PM at Airbnb, Amazon, Microsoft
> **YouTube**: [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MVkEY4P6QOQ](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MVkEY4P6QOQ)
> **Relevance**: Jason shares how to grow as a PM and navigate organizational changes

**Key Timestamps**:
- `07:45` - Growing from PM to leader
- `23:30` - Navigating organizational change
- `39:15` - Building influence and credibility
- `54:00` - Choosing the right role and org
- `1:09:30` - Career progression in product

**Key Insights**:
1. **Choose Orgs Where You Can Grow**: The best org for your growth isn't necessarily the most prestigious or highest-paying. It's the org where you'll have autonomy, mentorship, and scope. You learn faster when you own outcomes end-to-end and have leaders who coach you.
2. **Navigate Change Proactively**: Reorgs, leadership changes, strategy shifts—these are constants in tech. You can't control change, but you can control how you respond. Great PMs anticipate change, build relationships across the org, and position themselves to add value regardless of structure.
3. **Career Growth is Non-Linear**: The path from Sr. PM to Director to VP isn't a ladder—it's a jungle gym. You might move laterally to learn new skills. You might take a smaller role at a better org. You might leave PM to try something else, then come back. Prioritize learning over titles.

**Practice This Skill**:
> **Career Growth Audit**: Map your career trajectory over the past 3 years. What skills have you developed? What gaps remain? What's your next growth opportunity—inside your current org or elsewhere? Be honest with yourself about whether you're growing or stagnant.

**Discussion Questions**:
1. Are you growing in your current role, or are you stagnant? What's your next growth opportunity?
2. How do you navigate organizational change? Do you anticipate it or just react to it?

## Synthesis: Cross-Episode Themes

### Theme 1: Org Design Follows Strategy

**What the experts agree on**: Anneka Gupta, Casey Winters, and Gokul Rajaram all emphasize that org structure should be derived from strategy, not the other way around.

**Portable Skill**: Before designing or changing org structure, start with clarity on strategy: What problems are we solving? What outcomes matter? Then design orgs to execute that strategy.

**Career Application**: Directors and VPs are hired for their ability to design organizations that execute strategy. This is a core VP competency—start practicing now.

**Key Quotes**:
> "Org design follows strategy. If strategy changes, org design should follow." — Anneka Gupta

> "Great orgs give PMs end-to-end ownership of customer outcomes." — Casey Winters

### Theme 2: Management is Enabling Others, Not Doing It Yourself

**What the experts agree on**: Julie Zhuo, Camille Fournier, and Shishir Mehrotra all reinforce that the transition from IC to manager requires a fundamental mindset shift.

**Portable Skill**: Your success as a manager is measured by your team's output, not yours. Every decision should pass through this filter: Does this enable my team to be more effective?

**Career Application**: The managers who advance to Director and VP roles are the ones who build great teams, not the ones who do all the work themselves.

**Key Quotes**:
> "Management is about enabling others, not doing it yourself." — Julie Zhuo

> "The skills that made you a great PM won't make you a great manager." — Julie Zhuo

### Theme 3: Rituals Create Culture

**What the experts agree on**: Shishir Mehrotra, John Cutler, and Jason Shah all emphasize that culture is created through repeated practices and rituals.

**Portable Skill**: Design rituals intentionally. For each ritual, ask: What problem does this solve? Is this the best way to solve it? Who actually needs to participate? Kill rituals that don't serve clear purposes.

**Career Application**: Directors and VPs are culture setters. The rituals you design (or tolerate) become your team's culture. Be intentional.

**Key Quotes**:
> "Culture isn't values on a wall—culture is what you do repeatedly." — Shishir Mehrotra

> "Rituals must serve a purpose. If you can't articulate it, kill the ritual." — Shishir Mehrotra

## Action Plan: Applying This to Your Work

### Week 1: Audit Your Org Design
- [ ] **Map your current structure**: Create a visual map of your org structure. For each PM, note what outcomes they own and what decisions they can make.
- [ ] **Assess strategy alignment**: Review your product strategy. Does every team's work connect clearly to the strategy? Where are the misalignments?
- [ ] **Identify structural issues**: Where are there fragmented responsibilities? Unclear decision rights? Missing end-to-end ownership?
- **Output**: Clear understanding of structural issues and a plan to address them

### Week 2: Design or Improve Your Team Rituals
- [ ] **Audit existing rituals**: List every recurring ritual (meetings, updates, reviews). For each, identify the problem it solves and whether it's still effective.
- [ ] **Kill one bad ritual**: Choose one ritual that doesn't serve a clear purpose. Communicate why you're killing it and what will replace it.
- [ ] **Strengthen one good ritual**: Choose one ritual that's working well. How can you make it even more effective? Better prep? Clearer outcomes? Broader participation?
- **Output**: Healthier rituals and a culture that values intentional practices

### Week 3: Develop Your Management Skills
- [ ] **Management style reflection**: For each person you manage, assess what management style works best for them. Are you adapting your approach?
- [ ] **Feedback practice**: Give one person direct, actionable feedback this week. Use Radical Candor: care personally, challenge directly.
- [ ] **Growth conversation**: Have a career growth conversation with each person you manage. Where are they trying to grow? How can you help them get there?
- **Output**: Stronger management relationships and a team that feels supported in their growth

## Career Progression: From Sr. PM to VP Product

**Org design is the path to VP Product.** Here's how organizational leadership skills scale with seniority:

| Skill | Sr. PM | Director | VP Product |
|-------|--------|----------|------------|
| Org Scope | Lead one product team | Lead multiple product teams | Design entire product org structure |
| Hiring | Hire IC PMs | Hire PMs and managers | Hire Directors and build org |
| Management | May manage 1-2 PMs | Manage managers | Manage entire product leadership team |
| Rituals & Culture | Team rituals and culture | Product area culture | Company-wide product culture |

**Your path to VP**:
1. **Sr. PM**: Start managing 1-2 PMs. Learn the management basics. Build your management philosophy.
2. **Director**: Manage a team of PMs and possibly managers. Design org structure for a product area. Build rituals and culture at scale.
3. **VP**: Design the entire product org. Hire and manage Directors. Set company-wide product culture. Build the rituals that define how product works.

**Start now**: The org design and management skills you develop as a Sr. PM are the foundation for Director and VP roles. Don't wait for the title—start leading at the next level today.
