## Core Thesis: Why Product Leaders Should Become ICs

### The Leverage Equation Has Changed
- **AI multiplies IC productivity**: A single IC can now do the work of 10 people by managing a team of agents
- **The gap has narrowed**: High-performing ICs with AI tools can match the output of managers leading teams
- **Credibility requirement**: To work at AI-native companies (which most will be in 10 years), you need hands-on AI experience
- **Flatter organizations**: Companies will have more ICs/agents and fewer layers of management

### Why Now is the Time
1. **Get closer to the work**: Even if you've been a manager, you need to understand how AI changes execution
2. **Future-proof your career**: If you have 10-20 years left, you can't afford to skip this transformation
3. **Credibility with your team**: Managers who've done the work recently connect better with their ICs
4. **Hardest role to hire**: CEOs struggle to find heads of product who understand AI-native development

## How to Evaluate Companies (When Choosing Your Next Role)

### 1. Mission Alignment (Core Question: Do You Believe?)
- **Not about sexy slogans**: Do you believe in the pain this product solves?
- **Customer connection**: Have you talked to their customers? Do you care about serving them?
- **Day-to-day reality**: Will you enjoy the actual work, not just the brand?
- **Example**: Don't join an HVAC software company unless you're excited to talk to HVAC companies daily

### 2. Trajectory Over Status
- **Growing pie > fixed pie**: Fast-growing companies create opportunities, flat companies create politics
- **Example**: Friend chose "VP Product at Yahoo" over "PM at Google" in 2001 (based on status, missed trajectory)
- **Key metrics**:
  - Revenue growth
  - Customer growth
  - Market momentum
- **Venture capitalist mindset**: You're investing your career - evaluate like a VC would

### 3. Talent Density
- **Network effects**: Great people go on to do great things and pull you with them
- **Signal in interviews**: If every person you meet is the smartest person you've ever met, join
- **Example**: Gokul's Google connections â†’ Facebook â†’ Square opportunities
- **Test**: "1 out of X employees becomes a founder" - companies like Ramp have high ratios

### 4. Founder Quality
- **Dissatisfaction as superpower**: Best founders are never satisfied with current state
- **Long-term vision**: Can articulate 10-30 year vision that inspires you
- **Courage**: Willing to make bold bets (Example: Larry/Sergey were bigger risk-takers than executives)
- **Example**: Tony Hsieh at DoorDash - "We're going to reinvent local commerce" (not just food delivery)

### Don't Worry About Moats Early
- Moats are built over time by good people
- Focus on talent density - great teams create moats (Chrome at Google)
- "The score takes care of itself"

## Product Management Principles

### Customer Outcomes > Everything
**Key Shift**: Don't celebrate launches, celebrate customer behavior changes

**Framework**:
1. Define clear customer outcome (behavior change)
2. Map outcome to business metric
3. Create hypothesis for how feature moves the outcome
4. Run experiment on subset (5-10% of users)
5. Measure actual behavior change
6. If successful, roll out gradually
7. If unsuccessful, do root cause analysis (don't just move on)

**CEO's Only Question**: "Why are you shipping this feature? What customer outcome will it change?"
- If PM can't articulate clear hypothesis â†’ shut it down
- Better to ship fewer features that drive real outcomes

**Philosophical Test**: "If a feature shipped and no customer behavior changed, did it really ship?"

### Execution is Changing Dramatically

**Old Model**: Long PRD documents describing features in words

**New Model**:
- Very short context-setting document
- Plus a working prototype
- Show what to build through pictures/working code, not words

**PM Skills Convergence** (4 roles â†’ 2 people):
1. Analyst
2. Designer
3. Product Manager
4. Front-end Engineer

â†’ Will converge into 2 people doing all 4 roles (with AI tools)

**PM Superpower Specialization**:
- If you came from design â†’ PM + Designer
- If you came from engineering â†’ PM + Front-end Engineer
- If you came from analytics â†’ PM + Analyst

**Risk**: More execution time = less customer interview time
- Don't lose the art of talking to customers and extracting pain points
- Voice of customer remains critical PM responsibility

### Judgment & Influence Remain Human

**Judgment**: Given 100 ideas, which ones actually move the company forward?
- Prioritization based on customer impact
- Sequencing based on dependencies and resources
- What to cut to launch faster

**Influence**: How do you align the organization around priorities?
- Cannot be easily outsourced to machines
- Requires understanding people, politics, constraints

## Hiring Wisdom

### Don't Hire Head of Product Too Early
**Better approach**:
1. Promote from within
2. Start with senior PM who's worked 2-3 years at AI-native company
3. Let them do IC work first
4. Slowly grow the team
5. Promote internally to manager when needed

**Founder should manage PMs initially**:
- Learn what you actually need
- Best PMs are self-managed and create leverage for you
- Example: Founder fired 5 executives (CFO, CMO, CRO, CPO, etc.) because he didn't know what he wanted
- Only after doing their jobs for 6 months each did he learn who to hire

### Board Members & Executives: Try Before You Buy
- **Minimum advisor period**: 6 months before board seat
- **Gokul's examples**:
  - Coinbase: 6-7 months as advisor
  - Pinterest: Knew founder 15 years
  - Trade Desk: Knew founder 10+ years
- **Why**: These are one-way door decisions (like marriage)
- **Test**: Have them attend board meetings, work with team, add value before commitment

### Interview Process Quality = Talent Density
**Sign of great companies**: Interview process so tough you think you failed
- **Gokul's experience**: Thought he failed Google interview at every stage
- **De Shaw example**: "Hackers wanted" ad with no company name - ultimate filter
- **Result**: If you pass, you know everyone around you is exceptional

## Career Strategy & Success Patterns

### First 3-6 Months Make or Break You
**This period defines how you'll be remembered**:
- Good start â†’ Carries you for years
- Bad start â†’ Very hard to recover from

**What to do**:
1. **Show enthusiasm and energy**: Always volunteer for tough tasks
2. **Get a win within 60 days**: Ship something, change something, demonstrate impact
3. **Run toward vacuums**: "Opportunity shines where responsibility has been abdicated"
4. **Take work off your manager's plate**: Figure out how to give them leverage
5. **Be known for getting stuff done**: Not for questioning/blocking progress
6. **Ask for help**: Don't be too proud - leverage your team and manager

**Example**: Gokul's mindset joining Google
- "Nothing is beneath me"
- "Anything they ask, say yes with a smile"
- Remembered toughest moment at previous company: "Nothing at Google will be harder than that"

### Speed & Bias to Action

**Two-way door decisions**:
- Set up quick experiment with clear hypothesis
- Run it fast, get data, decide
- Don't argue for 6 weeks - test instead

**One-way door decisions**:
- Take time to decide carefully
- But there are very few of these

**Example**: AdSense launched in 3-3.5 months (fastest ever Google product)
- Sergey cut timeline from 6 months to 3 months
- Forced ruthless cutting of features
- Result: Known for speed and execution

### Be a "Barrel" Not Just "Ammunition"
- **Barrel**: Someone who when stuff goes in, it gets done and comes out the other side
- **Not solo hero**: Build relationships across company to leverage others
- **Social capital**: Built by actually getting things done + being fun to work with + contagious energy

### Diagonal Career Moves (Best Path)
1. **Vertical**: Same function, next level (PM â†’ Group PM)
2. **Horizontal**: Different function, same level (PM â†’ Designer)
3. **Diagonal** (BEST): Different function + higher level (PM â†’ Head of Product/Design/Engineering)

**Example path**:
- IC â†’ Manager of ICs â†’ Multi-function manager â†’ GM with P&L â†’ CEO

## Pricing & Business Model Strategy

### Outcome-Based Pricing Alignment
**Progression**: Seat-based â†’ Usage-based â†’ Outcome-based

**Key insight**: Can only go outcome-based if you control the outcome
- **Google example**: Stopped at cost-per-click (not conversion)
  - Why? They control the click, not what happens on your website after
- **Customer support AI**: Can do outcome-based ($ per resolved ticket)
  - Why? They control the entire resolution process
- **Cursor/IDEs**: Can't do outcome-based (engineer still writes the code)
- **Devon/autonomous agents**: Can do outcome-based (agent does the work)

**Rule**: Outcome pricing = what you can actually deliver and control

## Product Leadership & Culture

### SaaS Companies Danger: Revenue Booked â‰  Success
**Leading indicator**: Product usage (daily/weekly/monthly active users)
**Lagging indicator**: Revenue booked

**Problem**: "Shelfware" - Salesforce licenses bought but never used
- Half of licenses at some large companies go unused
- Revenue booked but customer will churn

**Better approach**: Track usage intensity as predictor of retention and expansion

### Best Products Have Minimal Features
**Great product test**: Can customers use it without training/manual?

**Example**: Square Point of Sale
- Traditional POS: Multi-day training required
- Square: Barista downloads from App Store and starts using immediately
- "No one trained me, I just started using it"

**Principle**: Cut features rather than add them
- Everyone can brainstorm 100 features
- Greatness = saying no to 99 of them

### Experimentation Culture (DoorDash Example)
- Never just "launch a feature" - run an experiment
- Every feature = hypothesis about customer behavior
- Test on 5-10% of users first (control group)
- If no meaningful behavior change â†’ don't roll out
- Must do root cause analysis when experiments fail
- Can't be lazy and move on to next experiment

**Interview signal**: DoorDash PM candidates judged on ability to:
1. Identify root causes
2. Unpack problems deeply
3. Create specific solutions for specific root causes
